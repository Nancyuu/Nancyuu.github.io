Detection of Fake News Based on Typical Machine Learning Models

Paper Title: Detection of Fake News Based on Typical Machine Learning Models
Author: Ruining Yang
Paper ID: GEBM-569
In this project, I employed advanced Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques, encompassing Sentiment Analysis, Named Entity Recognition, and Topic Modeling, to meticulously examine the distinguishing features of authentic and fabricated political news articles, sourced from a comprehensive Kaggle dataset. Utilizing Python for the analysis, I visualized the critical attributes of these articles through a combination of word clouds and graphical representations. Subsequently, I conducted a comparative evaluation of multiple machine learning models, encompassing Decision Tree, Logistic Regression, Random Forest, Multilayer Perceptron (MLP), and Naive Bayes, to assess their efficacy in discerning between legitimate and counterfeit news. Following a thorough analysis, I documented my findings and my paper was successfully accepted by 4th International Conference on Global Economy and Business Management (GEBM 2022) and published by international journal of “Lecture Notes in Economics, Management and Social Sciences" (ISSN: 2617-1031) in Clausius Scientific Press, and submitted to CPCI (ISTP), CNKI databases.

Data Science Project for Police Record

In this data-driven project, My teamate and I employed techniques such as RegEx and the PyPDF2 to clean and process a set of police records obtained through the Freedom of Information Act (FOIA). We also scraped and refined related public data from various sources to complement our dataset. The resulting cleaned data was then analyzed to uncover patterns and insights. To showcase our work and promote transparency, we created a dedicated website and a GitHub repository to publish the dataset and share our findings with the public. This project was supervised by Professor Alanna Hoyer-Leitzel at Mount Holyoke College. The primary objective of this project was to explore the potential of data science in enhancing transparency, promoting public awareness, and fostering data-driven decision-making in the realm of law enforcement and public safety.

Dockerized Minimal Notebook Environment Construction

This on-campus project offers a streamlined, Dockerized Jupyter notebook environment designed as a starting template for research projects. Developed under the guidance of Abby Drury from the Academic Technologies department in LITS, Sarah Oelker from the Educational Technology department in LITS, and Benjamin Gebre-Medhin from the Department of Sociology and Anthropology and the Data Science Committee, I constructed a robust Dockerized framework tailored specifically for minimalist Jupyter notebooks. Aimed at researchers who prefer a lean and efficient Python-only environment, this framework exclusively supports the Python programming language, guaranteeing minimal clutter and optimal performance.
The instruction of Dockerized container construction can be find in Wiki page.
